Section 1, Article V of the 1987 Philippine Constitution

Suffrage must be exercised by all citizens of the Philippines who are at least eighteen years of age and have resided in the Philippines for at least a year. The place where you propose to vote must be the place where you reside for at least six months prior to election.

Vote for the greater good

Philippines - a democratic country led by a president who is directly directed by the people. Wherein, the President is a powerful political figure that serves as both head of the state and the head of a government but only holds office for one six year term. We make the president where they are now. We make his/her cabinets by voting. We directly influence the decisions on policies and laws that affect our daily lives: such as education, healthcare, and infrastructure. By voting, we hold elected officials accountable and ensure that their actions reflect the will of the people.

To vote is to help strengthen democratic institutions and promote civic engagement and responsibility.

As per Article 21 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights

  • Everyone has the right to take part in the government of his country, directly or through freely chosen representatives.
  • Everyone has the right of equal access to public service in his country.
  • The will of the people shall be the basis of the authority of government; this will be expressed in periodic and genuine elections which shall be by universal and equal suffrage and shall be held by secret vote or by equivalent free voting procedures.

Every election hopes for better governance, more transparent government spending; improved services i.e., access to quality education, health, employment and livelihood opportunities, waste management and environmental protection; better infrastructure; greater attention to the fight against corruption, poverty alleviation, enabling programs for people’s participation. Each of these are the dreams of citizens whenever they cast a vote.

To be part of making a difference. To be able to participate in the creation of a nation that is aimed for the happiness of the next generations.

Understanding our duty to care helps strengthen the right to participatory governance through clean, honest, and orderly elections. We must all care, as our cherished freedoms and human rights depend on us!

Commission on Elections (COMELEC) ensured voting accessibility and participation of vulnerable groups in the 2022 national elections through Resolution No. 10761 wherein Emergency Accessible Polling Places (EAPPs) will be established in voting centers catering persons with disabilities, senior citizens, and heavily-pregnant women.

An EAPP is a room or makeshift polling place established on the ground floor of voting stations where persons with disabilities, senior citizens, and heavily-pregnant women may vote on Election Day. It can also be put outside but still close to the voting center. According to COMELEC, there was one EAPP in areas with 1-7 barangays; two for those 8-14 barangays; and, three for those with 15 and above barangays.

COMELEC was commended by the Commission on Human Rights (CHR) for ensuring the safety and well-being of vulnerable individuals. Preventing risks and discomforts that may impede them from suffrage. They made sure that facilities and materials are appropriate, accessible, user-friendly, and easy to understand, as well as, reasonable accommodation.

People who are not deserving would eventually be placed into position. Leading to bad governance - the polar opposite of good leadership. This entails systemic corruption and a lack of openness and accountability, arbitrary policy making, and the deception of those who are ruled.


As defined by the World Bank, lousy governance is a country with any or all indicators have values less than or close to -2.5. The following are the World Bank’s governance indicators:


  • Voice and accountability
  • Rule of law
  • Regulatory quality
  • Control of corruption
  • Government effectiveness

CORRUPTION

One significant obstacle to good governance in the Philippines is corruption. According to a survey of recent literature, the Philippines has a significant amount of corruption, ranging from minor bribes to grand corruption, patronage, and state control. Corruption has been combated through the implementation of legislative and institutional frameworks, as well as efforts by civil society organizations and the media. Donor agencies are also actively involved in increasing capacity to combat corruption in the Philippines. However, the success of these programs is far from certain. Many analysts feel that structural hurdles like entrenched corruption continue to impede anti-corruption efforts.
